Introduction

Tesla's mission since its inception in 2003 has been to provide mass-market electric cars (EVs)with a compelling customer value proposition. This comprises a long-range car with recharging flexibility, as well as an energy-efficient, low-cost, and high-performing vehicle that does not sacrifice design or functionality. This webpage explains Tesla's one-of-a-kind marketing strategy in China.

The design, production, and sale of fully electric vehicles are all part of Tesla's automotive business. Tesla's Model S, Model X, and Model Y premium sedans and SUVs target the premium car and SUV segments, while the Model 3 targets the mainstream vehicle market. Design, production, and sale of solar energy generation systems and energy storage solutions to industrial and commercial customers make up the energy generation and storage category. The company benefits from key expertise in powertrain engineering, vehicle engineering, innovative manufacturing, and energy storage, among other things.

Tesla's worldwide domination

Tesla has been leading the charge towards alternative energy in the passenger vehicle sector since going public in 2010. Tesla is currently regarded as a forward-thinking, exciting, and dominant player in the global vehicle business. Tesla's market worth hit $774 billion in 2021, making it the seventh most valuable firm in the United States.

Tesla's vehicle deliveries worldwide hit over 500,000 units in 2020, up 36% from 2019. Tesla's Model 3 is now the best-selling plug-in electric vehicle in the world. Model 3 car deliveries accounted for over 80% of total sales volume in the fourth quarter of 2019, duplicating the Model S and Model X products, which were originally geared at clients in the high-end sector of the market. With the Model 3, Tesla is now targeting a larger range of customers.

As we can see from the data below, Tesla has unquestionably dominated the electric vehicle industry, selling about 140,000 more vehicles than BYD, which came in second. This not only demonstrates Tesla's dominance in the electric vehicle market, but also the growing acceptability of electric vehicles by the general public.

Tesla’s China Entry

The Support By Chinese Government

The Chinese government's efforts to attract an influential international firm, in order to boost its image of market openness, were exemplified by Tesla's arrival into the country. China expects that Tesla will become an industry leader, promoting the localization of EV design and manufacture inside China's industrial and supply chain, with the ultimate goal of propelling China's EV industry to new heights.

Tesla's decision to invest in China was a watershed moment for the firm. Tesla made history by becoming the first foreign automaker to be granted full autonomy in China while maintaining 100 percent ownership. Tesla no longer has to worry about "forced technology (IP) transfers" of its patents, thanks to a recent amendment to China's Foreign Investment Law. Automakers such as Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Ford, Toyota, and other foreign carmakers have been pursuing this arrangement for years.

Factors Influencing Chinese People To Buy Electric Vehicles

Tesla After completing its Gigafactory in Shanghai in late 2019, China grew greatly in 2020 and beyond as a result of having local production capabilities. This is advantageous to the business for two reasons. First, because Tesla can avoid import duties on finished automobiles by manufacturing in China, and second, because its locally produced cars are eligible for government subsidies. China has been the ideal location for Tesla because it is a major supplier of automotive parts to the worldwide industry as well as a hub for electric mobility and smart grids.

From the data we can see apart from the environmental factor which is an inherent attribute of electric vehicles. Government subsidies and the price of energy are the main factors determining people's purchase of electric vehicles.

Tesla’s Supply and Value Chains

The Core component

Tesla's business model differs from that of other traditional automakers in that it controls the whole supply chain, from manufacturing to distribution. The ultimate goal of this strategy is to reduce manufacturing costs and the cost of goods supplied, ensuring the company's long-term viability. Unlike prior versions, the Model X and Model 3 are aimed at the mass market for electric automobiles. Tesla's supply chain management strategy prioritizes long-term growth through production, inventory management, and distribution.

Retail and Distribution

Tesla's business model encompasses every facet of retail and distribution. It owns all of its dealers and sales facilities, as well as handling vehicle repair and maintenance internally.

Traditional OEMs, on the other hand, rely on a multi-tiered sales network that includes both single-brand and multi-brand dealers. Tesla, in defiance of this entrenched structure, chose to promote its vehicles via the Internet. Making use of the so-called direct sales system. Tesla's direct sales model differs significantly from that of traditional franchised dealerships. In this way, the corporation not only owns the channel touch-points and data, but also the customer experience, which might lead to potential advantages for future product development.

Sales and Pricing

Some Tesla models are cheaper in China due to government subsidies for electric vehicles. Tesla, for example, lowered the price of a Chinese-made Model 3 by around 8% in 2020. The previous price was 271,500 yuan, but it was reduced to 249,900 yuan.

Most dealers are unsuited to online-only sales, but Tesla has done so successfully. The advantage of set pricing arises as a result of this sales model. This implies that the vehicle's retail pricing remains the same regardless of where you purchase it within the Tesla network. When you've gotten to the stage where you can price an EV at a fixed price, you can think about selling it online. This sets Tesla apart from the competition since other automakers would have to at least get to the stage where customers believe they are getting value from the brand. This pricing and distribution approach gives them the look and feel of an Apple product.

The Unique Strategy

The Market Strategy

Tesla's marketing strategy in China is unconventional. In truth, there isn't a marketing department at the corporation. Tesla's cutting-edge marketing strategy, according to Elon Musk, is "$0 Marketing Budget."

Despite the lack of a budget, there are few aspects that distinguish Tesla's Chinese marketing strategy. First and foremost, Tesla strives to give the finest customer service possible. The website has all of the information regarding cars, and the entire car-buying procedure may be completed online. During COVID-19, this is extremely critical.

Tesla's Chinese marketing strategy also includes a referral program, rather than paid advertising. People talk about their Tesla purchases and experiences, which attracts new buyers. Elon Musk's persona is unquestionably crucial to Tesla's Chinese marketing strategy. He has been one of the leading newsmakers in recent years and is active on social media.

Finally, in China, Tesla places a premium on transparency. Consumers' faith in a brand is increased when company representatives discuss what occurs if an issue arises.

Successful Marketing

Explaining the success

Tesla's success was largely dependent on timing and vision. In the mid-2000s, Tesla was one of only a few pure EV car manufacturers in the world, and it was likely the first to produce a reliable EV drivetrain and battery pack. The company was one of the first to anticipate that lithium-ion battery prices will continue to fall for many years. Tesla's hard charging nature, driven by CEO Elon Musk, as well as its distinct branding approach, helped it stand out from the competition and win devoted fans. Furthermore, Tesla's commercial and operational models are designed to address the major issues that Chinese EV buyers face.

Tesla’s Competitors

NIO, which went public in September 2018, Warren Buffett-backed BYD, SAIC Motor, a Volkswagen and GM partner, Geely Automotive Group, BAIC Group, and its subsidiary Beijing Electric Car Co. are among the electric vehicle manufacturers already operating in China. A set of electric car start-ups, including Byton and WM Motor Tech, is also on the rise in China.

Despite its dominance in electric vehicle sales in the United States, Tesla is trailing local competitors in China's large and lucrative market.
